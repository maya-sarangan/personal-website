    Who Actually Makes Rainbows?

    May 10, 2026

    My little brother invited his friend over for a playdate at our house. They ate snacks, played, and ended up watching a TV show called “My Little Pony”. It is a kids show featuring unicorns. I glanced over at the TV screen and watched a scene about a young unicorn sliding on the rainbow. The rainbow was solid, and included the color pink!(Since it is a kids show, I let it slide…) According to the character on the TV show, rainbows are made by pegasus ponies in a factory on Cloudsdale. Now, this made me wonder, how are rainbows actually made? Are they really made by pegasus ponies in a factory on Cloudsdale? These are the questions I will be answering today. Let’s go!

    Let me start by answering the million dollar question. Are rainbows made by pegasus ponies in a factory on Cloudsdale? NO! Of course not….I was just joking! Now, let’s get serious, how are rainbows actually made? They are made with water and sunlight! When you turn the hose on mist mode in summer, you see the colors of the rainbow, right? This is actually an example of water and sunlight taking action to make a rainbow (More specifically, the sun has to be behind you and the rain has to be in front of you). When there is sunlight and rain at the same time, they interact in such a way that the spectrum of colors found in a rainbow is born.

    Birth of a Rainbow

    Now, let’s dive in on what actually happens with the sunlight and the rain. We will take a singular droplet as an example. The following picture will demonstrate the entire flow:

    The sunlight hits the surface of the water droplet in a straight beam and refracts through the water droplet. Refraction is when the direction of the light beam is altered as it passes through the water droplet. As the light beam passes through, it splits into a spectrum, each displaying a different color of the rainbow(Sound familiar yet….?). This is called dispersion. The first point of contact is when the light beam touches the surface of the water droplet.

    Next, the light beams reflect against the opposite side of the water droplet. Reflection is when the light bounces back once it hits the edge of the water droplet. The beams reflect at approximately 40 degrees. So, the second point of contact is when the beams reflect from the other side of the water droplet.

    Lastly, the multi - colored beams refract once more and exit the water droplet. This is the third point of contact.

    This is how the rainbow is born.

    Two People - Two Rainbows

    A fun fact about rainbows is that people actually see the rainbow differently depending on where they are standing! If you’re standing next to your friend, looking at a rainbow, both of you will perceive the rainbow differently. This happens because of many different reasons such as the personal center, different droplets, and the 40 - 42 degree angle rule.

    The personal center rule is actually quite simple. Since the sun is behind you, the rainbow will always be opposite from the sun, which points the shadow at the center of your head(also known as the “antisolar point”). Thus, when you take a step forward, the center of the rainbow moves with you.

    Now let’s go to different droplets. Since we already know that a rainbow is made up of billions of rain droplets refracting and reflecting light, the set of droplets that make up the rainbow you see will never be the same as the set of raindroplets that make up the rainbow that your friend sees.

    Finally, let’s go to the 40 - 42 degree angle rule. the way you see the rainbow also depends on the angle from which you are looking at it. For example, if you’re looking at the rainbow at a 42 degree angle, you will see the red spectrum. And if you’re looking at a 40 degree angle, you will see the violet spectrum!

    The real shape

    Another interesting fact is that rainbows aren’t actually the semicircle’s we think they are. They are actually circular! The only reason we cannot see the whole thing is because our view is blocked by the ground. If there was nothing in our way, we would be able to see the whole rainbow! If you were on an airplane and there was a rainbow, you would actually be able to see the whole rainbow!

    Double Rainbow and Moonbow

    One little known fun fact is that there are actually rainbows called double rainbows and moonbows(I wonder what My little Pony’s explanation for that is…. ). The science behind them is actually quite simple.

    Double rainbows are formed when sunlight reflects twice inside the water droplet before exiting. The second reflection causes the colors in the outer rainbow to appear reversed.

    Now, let’s go to moonbows. Normally rainbows are made with sunlight and water droplets, but moonbows are made with moonlight and water droplets and the moon, like the sun, must be behind you and the rain should be in front of you. The sky must be clear and free of pollution for the moonbow to clearly appear. The colors of a moonbow are actually the same as your average rainbow, but since moonlight is fainter than sunlight, the moonbow appears to be white.

    Questions

    I’m sure that by now you must have a lot of questions such as:

    1. Why are rainbows curved?
    2. How do the colors arrange themselves so neatly to make the rainbow?
    3. Why are there only seven colors in a rainbow?

    Well my friend, I will now answer those mind - boggling questions! Let’s go!

    Why Rainbows Are Curved?

    Rainbows are curved because water droplets are round and refract sunlight at an angle at about 42 degrees. This creates a cone of light with your eyes at the tip of the cone, giving you a curved rainbow.

    Color arrangement

    The secret behind the colors stacking so nicely is hidden in the water droplet’s elevation. Light from droplets higher in the sky reaches your eyes as red, while droplets slightly lower appear orange, then yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet. The colors don’t overlap because, the wave length of each color is different(red is the longest and violet is the shortest) so each color bends at a slightly different angle. Because of this, the colors cannot overlap even if they tried.

    Why 7?

    Now, let’s go to the last question. The explanation for why there are only seven colors in a rainbow is actually funny. There are actually infinitely many shades in a rainbow. Isaac Newton originally divided the spectrum into seven named colors because he liked the connection between seven colors and the seven notes in music.
